Back in September we sent all the sailors, except for around 70 crew members, on special trains from Murmansk to Leningrad to travel home to Germany. This was necessary to ensure that we do not run out of food supplies. But our oil supplies for heating and lighting only last until the end of October. We are incredibly lucky that a German tanker docks in Murmansk at the end of September and we are given permission to fill up.

On November 30th 1939 Russia attacks Finland and has been at war ever since. I want to leave with the ship and am asking Germany about a return.

On December 6th 1939 we finally get permission. They send us a few more men to support us. But we will only have 124 men on board in total, so we are thinly staffed. The crew is immediately banned from going ashore.

On December 10th 1939, at 2 a.m., the anchor is lifted. The S.S. Bremen leaves the port of Murmansk.
There is a thick snowstorm. Visibility is poor. The ship moves silently through the sea so as not to be recognized. It is not until around 11 o'clock that it becomes light. The snow is now so thick that we can no longer see anything. The danger of the cliffs and coast is too great. We lower the anchor almost silently. But we must get out of here as quickly as possible, otherwise we will be betrayed tomorrow morning. More than an hour passes before the squall passes. We raise the anchor again and head full steam out into the Atlantic. Fortunately, it gets dark again at 3 o'clock in the afternoon. When we break through the English exclusion zone, we stay 40 miles from the coast. Whenever the lookout post spots ships in the distance, we change course. The ship is sailing at full speed.

A searchlight flashes in the darkness. This is undoubtedly an English ship. We are at least 10 miles away and are sailing with our lights off. We hope that the Englishman has not seen us. We change course.

On Tuesday morning, at around nine o'clock, we are abeam of the Kattegat. The German minefield is now drawing closer, which will give us protection from English submarines. An aircraft is heading towards us from the south. We are relieved to see that it belongs to the German Air Force. At around eleven o'clock, it happens: we receive the report that a British submarine is four miles ahead. We immediately change course and now sail in a zigzag pattern. The unpredictability this creates, but also our speed, are our strengths. In principle, we could also run over a submarine with the S.S. Bremen and thus endanger it, because our ship has 15 bulkheads with 16 watertight compartments, giving it an unbeatable safety factor.

At twelve o'clock, we arrive in the German minefield. We can end the zigzag course. A German outpost boat is coming towards us. We stop the engines. The sea pilot, who is coming with a German torpedo boat, comes on board the S.S. Bremen. Only now does he realize who we are. We are so well camouflaged by the grey colour. The sea pilot's German torpedo boat now takes over our protection, as we still might be attacked.

At 8:30 a.m. we reach the mouth of the river Weser and are safe from British submarines due to the shallow water. At 9:18 a.m., 22 nautical miles from Bremerhaven, we drop anchor. The next morning we raise anchor. A tug from our shipping company accompanies us to our home port of Bremerhaven. We are finally home.

A few words about Captain Ahrens:

He retired in 1941. From 1949 to 1953 he was a member of the 1st German Bundestag. In Bremerhaven the Kommodore-Ahrens-Straße (street) was named after him. Due to Ahrens' Nazi past (he was a member of the NSDAP), additional explanatory signs were added to the street signs in 2017.

The sinking of the S.S. Bremen:



The ship was painted in camouflage colors over the next few months and converted into troop transport No. 802 for Operation Sea Lion. This operation was about the German invasion of Great Britain, which was never carried out.

The British Royal Air Force heard of this new mission and therefore tried to sink the ship. The attempts were unsuccessful.

On March 16th, 1941, a fire broke out in the wood-paneled dining room of the S. S. Bremen, which had been converted into a mattress storage room. It spread throughout the entire ship and got out of control. Since the S. S. Bremen was leaning heavily towards the quay, it was flooded so that it could right itself and sink in the shallow waters of the Weser. This made it easier to recover the machines.

Investigations revealed that the 15-year-old sailor Walter Schmidt had set the fire in revenge for a slap in the face that a superior had given him. Schmidt was subsequently executed.

In 1946, the Bremen was towed to a sandbank three miles upstream, where it was gradually dismantled and scrapped from 1952 onwards.
